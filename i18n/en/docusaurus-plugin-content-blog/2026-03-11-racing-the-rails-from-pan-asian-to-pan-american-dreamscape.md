## Racing the Rails: From Pan-Asian to Pan-American Dreamscape 🚄
<p className="sj2">
These past few days, I've been dreaming frequently, waking up with only bizarre fragments left. Except for this dream. Although the scenery has blurred, the inner turmoil and struggle are etched as clearly as a knife's edge, compelling me to record it.
</p>
<!-- truncate -->
<p className="sj2">
The dream's origin might stem from the few marathons I've participated in for fun over the years. My dream's starting point was set in Pingxiang, Guangxi. I stood there with a running friend who, in reality, has similar race times and has run many marathons alongside me. However, this time our goal wasn't 21 or 42 kilometers. It was to follow the route of the Pan-Asian Railway, measuring Vietnam, Laos, Thailand, and Malaysia with our own feet, traversing the entire Indochina Peninsula all the way to Singapore.

But the subconscious in my dream didn't let me indulge in blind passion. Instead, deep anxiety born of reason instantly seized me. I was acutely aware that this land is not peaceful. The ongoing conflict in Myanmar since 1948, Laos's past status as one of the least developed countries with its infrastructure shortcomings, and the bloody history between China and Vietnam... these geopolitical realities transformed into an intangible, heavy pressure in the dream.

I even began to fear: as an unprotected pedestrian traversing on foot, what if I encountered danger in this foreign land where I don't speak the language? Even if I were lucky enough to meet a righteous police officer, I could only pray they understood a little English, relying on our half-baked vocabulary and gestures to survive.

This psychological drain defeated us far earlier than any physical fatigue in our legs. After lingering in Pingxiang for a long time, weighing the difficulties and dangers akin to a "journey to the West for scriptures," we ultimately chose to give up and boarded a return flight.

"Go to the American continent," I thought to myself.

When the flight finally landed at the familiar airport, the long journey and the gap between expectation and reality had already exhausted the initial passion. My running friend looked at my still-unwilling expression and shook his head wearily: "North America is certainly great, but I'm afraid I can't accompany you." The exhaustion from the round trip from Pingxiang had drained his strength to cross the Pacific again. Watching his departing figure, I knew in my heart that only my own obsession remained.

Since the Indochina Peninsula left such lingering fear, my subconscious urgently sought a safe container for this grand journey. North America seemed like a natural safe harbor. As if grasping at a life-saving straw, I immediately started frantically scrolling on my phone, searching for flights to Vancouver, Seattle, New York, Boston. "All off-peak tickets, let's go now." I didn't even leave the terminal; I walked directly to the international departure gate. The logic in dreams is always adorably bizarre. I completely disregarded the hassle of a U.S. visa and stood righteously in line at the gate for the flight to America.

The line for the U.S.-bound flight was crowded. Standing in the queue, my heart raced, my mind filled with fantasies about the New World. However, . My family, who had been waiting for me outside the airport for a long time without seeing me emerge, eventually found their way to the departure gate. Faced with my sudden, crazy plan, they were naturally extremely displeased, directly calling it an extremely irresponsible, impulsive act.

There was no imagined intense argument or physical struggle. They just stood there quietly, their eyes filled with worry and incomprehension towards my irresponsible, unprepared behavior. I had prepared a whole speech, wanting to tell them how much I wanted to go, planning to use the high ticket cancellation fees as a shield. But the moment I met their gaze, I suddenly deflated.

Reason returned in the dream—I suddenly realized I hadn't even applied for a U.S. visa. This seemingly magnificent cross-continental journey was ultimately just a fantasy built on sand. I didn't persist. In the vast terminal, I compromised. At the moment of giving up, there was no anger, only a faint, lingering sadness spreading in my heart. Carrying this sense of loss, I turned and left the airport with my family.

In a state of calm regret, I woke from the dream.

The morning light outside the window gradually brightened. In my grogginess, I subconsciously checked the price of a ticket to New York's JFK airport. Indeed, as in the dream, it wasn't particularly expensive. But this post-awakening action made me realize that what my subconscious truly craves might not be running, but rather the desire to be like those travel bloggers, sitting on a train, listening to the roar of the rails, and traversing an entire continent with single-minded focus.

Coincidentally, on this very day, news emerged that the K27/28 international train might resume service. Although it's not bound for the Indochina Peninsula or America, it still serves as a conclusion that brings a touch of reality to my absurd yet vivid dream.

Postscript, March 12, 2026: On the evening of March 10, China State Railway Group officially announced the [resumption of two-way operation for the China-North Korea international passenger train starting March 12](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/XuTp9QJQv8z6LAIQQ_58kQ).
