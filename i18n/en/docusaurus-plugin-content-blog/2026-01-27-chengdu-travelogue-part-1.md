## Chengdu Travelogue (Part 1): Folklore and Cuisine
<p className="sj2">
For a long time, I had focused my gaze on China's plains and coastlines, having almost never ventured into the southwestern hinterland of my homeland, or even the central regions. To me, that was a folded-up map.

### Why Choose Chengdu?

The reasons are multifaceted. If one were to look for an absolute "world city" in mainland China, Shanghai would undoubtedly be the glamorous first choice—it is the reception room facing outward. But if one were to turn their gaze to the southwest, seeking a city that can hold its ground, then Chengdu is the only answer. The popular jest, "We only know of Chengdu Province, not Sichuan City," while playful, speaks to this city's unique gravitational pull and presence within the basin. Driven by this inquiry and curiosity, I set foot in this humid basin for the first time.

The texture of a city is often first encountered through its transportation.

Strolling the streets, my first impression of Chengdu was not a singular "Bashu flavor," but a rough and vivid sense of mixture. It was common to see Tibetan people in long robes or monks in kasayas weaving through the bustling crowds, speaking languages I couldn't understand. Passing by a subway station in the morning, a nearby Xinjiang naan vendor was loudly playing a Uyghur-language program—a high-pitched, fervent sound.

I was initially surprised, but upon reflection, this is Chengdu. As the absolute "big brother" of Sichuan, it sits at the back of a host of ethnic minority autonomous prefectures in western Sichuan like Aba, Garzê, and Liangshan. It is the first stop for countless ethnic minorities descending from the plateaus and mountains into the modern metropolis. This is the bottom of the basin, and also home to countless people from other lands.

As night falls, this sense of mixture reaches its climax in the squares. Passing by several public square dance venues, it was like a microcosm of cultural slices: some danced to the strong rhythms of popular group dances; others danced ballroom dances with reserved steps to elegant waltzes; and in a more secluded corner, I even heard music with a strong Uyghur style, dancers stepping to a unique beat—in Chengdu's night, everyone finds their own rhythm.

This inclusive, vibrant atmosphere is not only embodied in the people but also permeates the city's very breath. If the multi-ethnic cohabitation is a gift of the geographical environment, then this city's obsession with "health preservation" and "herbs" is a deep-seated cultural consciousness.

The atmosphere of Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) in Chengdu is the strongest I've ever seen. Perhaps it's because I stayed near a TCM hospital, which itself carries a certain "survivorship bias," but even setting that aside, the visible construction is evidence enough: subway station pillars are decorated with passages from the *Huangdi Neijing*, and even in bustling scenic spots and stations, TCM-related products always have a place. Regardless of actual efficacy, this "herbal" foundation is indeed profound.

### Medicine and Dao Share the Same Origin

The roots of TCM are deeply embedded in Daoist culture, and Mount Qingcheng, not far from Chengdu, is precisely a Daoist paradise.

The Daoist temples on Mount Qingcheng are not isolated but connected one after another, with incense smoke diffusing in the humid air. If Shanghai's Jing'an Temple and Suzhou's Xuanmiao Temple represent an "urban seclusion" for worldly cultivation, then Mount Qingcheng preserves the traditional Daoist aloofness of "withdrawing from the world." Above a thousand meters, clouds and mist drift between the treetops and pagoda tops, coupled with the biting cold temperature at the summit, walking there truly gives one the illusion of "floating as if detached from the world, ascending to immortality like a feathered being." Here, seeking the Dao is not just about worshipping deities; it's more like a dialogue of harmony between heaven and humanity.

However, the cultivation of Chengdu people is not solely about asceticism. Descending from Mount Qingcheng and shedding that layer of ethereal air, Chengdu people can immediately plunge into the most intense worldly pleasures—like that pot of boiling red oil.

I had long heard of Chengdu hotpot's fame, but my previous experience mostly came from "moderate" chains like Haidilao, adapted to suit various regional tastes. It wasn't until I actually sat on a Chengdu street that I realized my understanding of "spicy" was biased.

### Personally, I believe that from the essence of its craft, the soul of Chengdu hotpot lies not in "spiciness," but in "oil."

The pot base that arrived was startling: the proportion of red oil was absurdly high. Before heating, the oil was almost fifty-fifty with the broth, if not more. And with the dipping sauce, Chengdu people take "oil" to its ultimate conclusion—minced garlic, oyster sauce, and finally, a small ladle of specially prepared sesame oil must be poured over, instantly releasing a pungent, fragrant aroma.

I once wondered, how do locals treat such intense spiciness as commonplace? Upon reflection, this might be the wisdom of "heavy oil." The thick oil coats the ingredients and also the stomach lining. It acts like a gentle buffer, neutralizing the sharp sting of capsaicin and reducing the potential "suffering" one's body might endure the next day. Although from a modern nutritional perspective, excessive oil intake is a burden on the stomach and indeed caused some intolerance for my out-of-town digestive system, in that moment, the oil truly tamed the fire.

Leaving Chengdu, I looked back at the city and found it full of fascinating contradictions. It possesses both the cool, ethereal air of Mount Qingcheng shrouded in clouds and mist, and the worldly, fiery heat of boiling red oil in hotpot. It is not only the geographical center of the southwest but also a vast container. Here, ethereal air and worldly smoke are not opposites but two sides of the same life. This is perhaps Chengdu's most authentic folklore: holding a scroll to seek the Dao in one hand, and raising chopsticks to blanch meat in the other, dissolving all conflicts in ease and comfort.
