## The Afternoon of Stealing Fire and the Unforeseen AI Wave
<p className="sj2">
Recently, a retro cream-colored silent keyboard was added to my desk. Fingertips tapping on it, without the noise of the previous keyboard, actually let my thoughts drift away. This gentle hue is much like the yellowed old keyboards from elementary school computer classes. If a scroll mouse were also at hand now, time would seem to flow back to that ancient computer lab. Although by then we no longer had to wear shoe covers when going to the computer room, the mottled notices on the wall still stubbornly clung to the old rules.
</p>
<!-- truncate -->
<p className="sj2">

My computer enlightenment teacher was Teacher D. He was tall and thin, and his voice, unlike the deep and powerful tones of ordinary male teachers, was always soft-spoken. This gentle voice, intertwined with the day-in, day-out hum of the switch in the corner of the computer room, had an indescribable harmony and tranquility.

I still remember an ordinary day when Teacher D softly asked everyone if they were interested in coming to the computer room for training during free time after lunch. Naturally, I signed up with great joy. It was preparation for the county's elementary school student information competition. We used a software called "PC Logo 4.0" (Little Turtle Drawing), typing commands to control a little turtle on the screen to crawl out specified patterns on the canvas. Although I ultimately didn't win gold or silver in the county competition, this slowly crawling little turtle genuinely pushed open the first door to the world of computer programming for me.

The computer room in the afternoon was quiet and peaceful, with only a few of us students who stayed behind. The so-called "training" was more like a "free exploration" under the teacher's shelter. The competition questions were mostly variations of fixed patterns, and the contest each semester was essentially a speed race testing memory. Once we skillfully reproduced the key points on the exam, the remaining time became our carnival. In the virtual system, we could freely explore and change settings without worrying about serious consequences—at worst, we could restart the computer, and everything would be restored to its original state. This was completely different from the "Electronic Assembly" I later encountered, where burning out a resistor or causing a motor to "die of exhaustion" from high voltage was common. To check for damaged electronic components, I even asked the teacher about using a multimeter and troubleshooting circuits.

For elementary school students, exploration on the computer was always filled with magical allure. Starting from bringing up the Spider Solitaire and 3D Pinball games that were disabled by default in the Control Panel, our courage grew. Later, having our own USB drives, I began secretly transferring games from home to school. In that isolated LAN island with the WAN port network cable unplugged, who wouldn't want a thrilling round of CS 1.6, or dive into the worlds of *Grand Theft Auto: Vice City* and *San Andreas* for a wild ride? Back then, I particularly enjoyed "committing all sorts of crimes" in *Vice City*. As a player who could memorize several common cheat codes, I enjoyed star-like treatment among the GTA players. They would eagerly pull me over to their computers to spawn luxury cars, weapons, and endless wealth, then recklessly crash into NPCs on the screen.

Of course, in my youthful ignorance, I also did some laughably foolish things. The most classic was solemnly copying the 2KB "shortcut" from my home computer desktop onto a USB drive, bringing it to school to show off to classmates, and together imagining how much fun we'd have in computer class. Naturally, it couldn't be opened at all, leaving me wishing I could find a hole to crawl into under everyone's gaze. Learning from that mistake, I started exploring online, learning to find portable versions, configure old .NET environments, and even dabbled in IPv4 and IPv6 settings. Although I was far from understanding or being proficient, I finally had some insights into common problems classmates faced. Don't ask about the principles; as long as it runs, it's fine. This skill made me quite the star among my classmates.

However, the joy of the LAN ultimately had its limits. To keep us focused on practice during class, whenever Teacher D wasn't in the computer room, he would always unplug the WAN port RJ45 connector from the switch in the cabinet and lock the cabinet. This way, we could only play local multiplayer at most, unable to roam freely in the vast sea of various mini-games and video websites.

But how could I stop there? I knew the secret to network connection lay solely in that little RJ45 connector. As long as I could break through that lock, reconnect the network cable, the vast internet would open up to us again. I pondered for a long time until one day I happened to glance at the answer that had been right at hand all along—a small pair of scissors on Teacher D's podium. Perhaps they were tools he kept handy for repairing computers and network cables. The scissors were just the right size. Inserting them into the keyhole, slightly opening the handles, and gently shaking them left and right would loosen the lock core. Repeating this a few times, it took less than a minute for the cabinet door to open for me. When the connector clicked into the switch, I shouted in my heart: "Internet, I'm back!"

After each brief session of satisfying my internet craving, I would carefully restore everything to its original state. But during those moments of connection, under the eager gazes of my classmates, I felt like Prometheus stealing fire from the gods, bringing the light of the network to the believers on earth. That feeling of being surrounded and cheered by the crowd was probably no less than that of a CEO ringing the bell at a stock exchange listing.

Unfortunately, this lock-picking act was eventually discovered. Teacher D didn't scold me harshly but earnestly told me not to pick the lock anymore, as it would eventually break if I continued. From then on, he seemed to understand that I would try every means to connect to the network. So, when he wasn't in the computer room, he never unplugged that network cable again.

This period during computer class greatly influenced my hobbies and interests. From then on, I was no longer limited to the school's computer textbooks. With my father's help, I tried to self-learn "wild techniques" like BIOS, cmd command line, and Task Manager. Although these were just superficial knowledge, they became an extremely rare accumulation for me. By university, relying on this purely wild and incomplete knowledge system, I became the "IT support" in my dorm. Reinstalling systems, replacing hard drives, installing drivers—all were no problem. Even when encountering tricky issues, a quick search and review could solve them. When classmates joked about why I didn't major in computer science or just become a network administrator, I always smiled and replied: "My aspirations lie elsewhere; playing with computers is just my hobby."

Looking back from today's vantage point, as long as one has the will, the barrier to further advancing computer skills is almost zero for anyone. With the assistance of various code-proficient generative AIs (GenAI), expanding knowledge boundaries has become effortless. For those AIs themselves developed by engineers from major tech companies, computer knowledge is merely their simplest, most familiar underlying language (one could even say, the computer is GenAI itself).

However, whenever I think of this, an indescribable pain and regret well up in my heart. Teacher D can no longer personally experience the wonders of today's AI. About five or six years ago, he passed away due to an unfortunate battle with digestive system cancer; the exact year is a bit fuzzy in my memory. But he ultimately couldn't cross the river of time to experience the first wave of generative AI led by ChatGPT at the end of 2022.

I still vividly remember the instant melancholy and heartache that hit me when an elementary school classmate informed me of Teacher D's obituary. He was one of my most beloved teachers, yet also the earliest among all the teachers who have taught me to pass away. Now, whenever family or friends call me to fix a computer, my mind uncontrollably flashes back to that noon—when the tall, thin figure was absent, and I was squatting in front of the cabinet, using a small pair of scissors to pick the lock to the vast world.

Since then, I have never heard any news about Teacher D again. I only know he left behind a wife and daughter. At the end of this article, I sincerely wish them good health and smooth sailing through the years. 🙏
