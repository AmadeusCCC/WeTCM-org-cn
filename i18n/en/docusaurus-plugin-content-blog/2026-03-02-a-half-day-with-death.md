## A Half Day Drinking with Death☠️🍺

<p className="sj2">

A few days ago, I had a dream where I **dreamed of my own death date**. It wasn't born of fear; I've always been at peace with death. The starting point of life is birth, and the endpoint is death—an indisputable law. All we can do is sketch different curves between these two points to realize our respective values.
</p>
<!-- truncate -->
<p className="sj2">

The dream began with a prophet-like figure appearing, calmly informing me that I would pass away at noon the day after tomorrow. I was somewhat surprised but quickly came to terms with it. For a moment, I was even privately curious: what would it actually feel like when death truly arrived? I pondered for a while but found no answer.

I pressed the prophet further, asking if he knew the death dates of others. The prophet replied that my good friend Ding would depart about an hour before me. My curiosity grew—not because I harbored any ill will towards my dear friend, but genuinely wondering what it would be like if two people were destined to bid farewell to this world in such close succession.

I soon told Ding about this. After hearing it, Ding chuckled and said, "That's normal. Since death is predestined, there's no need to resist it." He was remarkably optimistic about it, as if discussing a lighthearted joke. So, Ding suggested we go for a drink, and we headed to a tavern to share some Dukang wine. My alcohol tolerance isn't great, but I'm usually hard to push to the limit because my body instinctively resists alcohol before getting completely drunk. But this time, as soon as I felt slightly tipsy, an unusual sensation washed over me.

My heart started racing uncontrollably. "Tachycardia?" was my first thought. I immediately took medication, but there was no relief. "Could it be primary hypertension?" Yet there was no family history of it... Just as I was puzzling over it, a shock-like dizziness struck me, and I lost consciousness.

When I woke up again, I was in a hospital room. The room was nothing special, just ordinary furnishings, not an ICU or any intensive care unit. I struggled to get up, managed with some effort to leave the room, and found Ding in the next room. I walked in; he was lying on the bed with his eyes closed, resting.

"Such an acute onset of primary hypertension probably isn't easy to treat," I said, looking at Ding.

Ding smiled and said, "It seems the prophecy is coming true."

I gazed at his face, thinking that since he couldn't even stand up, his condition was likely worse than mine. Ding's head was deeply sunk into the pillow, showing no intention of getting up. Leaning on the bed rail, I chatted with him. Ding told me he had been suffering from a certain illness for years, and his condition had been poor these past few days—not because of the drinking, but due to accumulated exhaustion that had finally worn his body down.

I have always seen Ding as a role model. Outside the ivory tower, he taught me so much, making me truly understand responsibility and choice. It's not that I hadn't heard these grand principles before, but the deepest insights came from my experiences with Ding. Leaning on the rail, I couldn't help but feel a deep sigh and regret. If it were me before meeting Ding, I probably would have been in tears by now.

Ding sensed my despondency. Propping himself up with his hands, he slowly sat up and suggested accompanying me for a walk around the area. I gladly agreed. In the past, we often shared our thoughts while strolling. Just like in my memories, Ding remained profound, his knowledge and perspective far surpassing mine. Therefore, whenever I faced confusion in life, I always turned to him for advice.

  ### Ding asked me what the specific time of the prophet's predicted death was.

"This afternoon," I said helplessly. "Your time is an hour earlier than mine." I dared not meet his eyes, afraid of seeing lingering sorrow on that familiar face.

"It's still morning; there's half a day left. Remind me when the time comes, and we'll return to our rooms early to avoid troubling the medical staff," Ding replied calmly.

I had to agree. After all, according to the prophet, I had one more hour of life than him. What would happen in that extra hour was anyone's guess. What if I didn't end up dying? Then Ding's departure would undoubtedly be a huge blow to me. But since he himself wasn't afraid, why should I worry so much?

So, for the next half day, we took a small stroll around the hospital. Like one last tour of the campus before graduation, we reminisced about the past and discussed the future—a future we were destined not to partake in. We each made our wills, clearly arranging our affairs to leave no doubts for the living.

We were like close friends seeing each other off at a train station, knowing we could no longer walk side by side, only watching each other fade into the distance at the ticket gate. When we occasionally looked back, we only hoped to recognize in each other's eyes the life curves we had once sketched together.

That morning's conversation was incredibly smooth, as if we had never interacted so harmoniously before. Nearing noon, Ding said he was a bit hungry and wanted to go back for lunch, after which he would prepare to meet his end. "I don't want to be a hungry ghost," he joked. How many patients in the world depart without knowing hunger or fullness? Probably countless. In life, eating is paramount; being able to have a full meal at the end is a fitting conclusion.

After the meal, Ding returned to his bed, finding a comfortable position as his final enjoyment in life. He called me to his bedside and instructed, "I don't want you to make a fuss when the time comes. Once a person is dead, they're dead; nothing can change that. Everything I wanted to say, I've laid out clearly this morning. I've already laid bare my heart."

I held his hand tightly, reluctant to leave.

"You still haven't shaken off that habit of being too indecisive. What's there to cling to? The old must leave eventually, and the new will always come. I think even though my body will perish, my thoughts and will have been recorded in this world." He waved his hand, signaling me to leave.

I walked to the door, unable to resist looking back at him.

"I'm afraid you'll be too tense when the time comes, and your expression will be unpleasant. You should go back first," he said, tucking his hand under the covers, adjusting his head position, and closing his eyes. I had no choice but to retreat to my own room and await the arrival of my death date.

The death date arrived as scheduled. The first to notify me were the medical staff from next door. They already knew about my relationship with Ding and called me to see him off one last time. They lifted Ding onto a gurney, and I helped push it while holding the rail. Walking at the front, I knew clearly that this was one of the few times I could lead the way for Ding.

I accompanied Ding, along with the medical staff, to the first floor. The medical school's vehicle had been waiting downstairs for some time. According to the will, Ding's body would be donated to the medical school for anatomical research—a destination we had agreed upon together,既不浪费资源，也能为未来的医学之路燃一把微光。

Watching Ding being wheeled into the vehicle, my mind replayed his teachings to me time and again. The medical school's vehicle didn't start immediately; I knew they were waiting for me. Leaning against the wall, I no longer cared to check the wall clock. For me at that moment, the exact hour and minute of my death no longer mattered. I felt an intense fatigue. After following the gurney downstairs and jogging a short distance, the tachycardia seemed to flare up again. I wanted to go back and rest a bit; no matter what, I should meet the end of life with a good spirit.

Leaning against the wall, I slowly walked back to the ward. Ding's bed was already empty, tidied up exceptionally neatly. If Ding could have seen it, he would have been very pleased. I smiled slightly and decided to return to my own room to rest.

Not long after, I felt myself "wake up." My mind was somewhat confused and sluggish, as if my whole body were soaked in warm water, but without the pain of suffocation or hypoxia.

  ### I thought, my death date has arrived.

Such a peaceful passing could be considered a good death. I felt a strange sense of solace.

So, like Ding, I was wheeled onto a gurney and taken away by the medical school's vehicle.

Only this time, there was no one ahead to lead the way for me.
