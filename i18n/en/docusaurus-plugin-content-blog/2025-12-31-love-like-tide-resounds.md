## When Love Like Tide Resounds Again
<p className="sj2">
A few weeks ago, bored out of my mind, my fingers unconsciously swiped across the phone screen. It was a long-sealed playlist, like a forgotten private museum. I've always been allergic to the noisy "viral hits" of the moment, preferring those old songs polished smooth by time.
</p>
<!-- truncate -->
<p className="sj2">

Suddenly, Jeff Chang's "Love Like Tide" crashed into view.

I pressed play. The intro began, and the floodgates of memory instantly burst open. For others, this song might be about midnight tears over a white moonlight, but for me, it has nothing to do with romance. It's only about that senior year of high school filled with exam papers, about a perpetually silent figure.

He was a male classmate, solidly built with a round face, like a Maitreya Buddha. It was the last birthday party before the college entrance exam, and he had signed up for this song, "Love Like Tide," on the program list. Unfortunately, the bell for the class meeting that day was like a ruthless rest symbol, cutting off his path to the stage. Later, we all scattered to the four corners of the country, and I never got to hear his version. To this day, I still don't understand why this heavyweight contender loved this bitter love song so much. In that youthful early summer, into whose heart did he want to sing this tide?

There are a thousand Hamlets in a thousand readers' eyes. And in the script of my senior year, there was only one protagonist—my senior year math teacher and homeroom teacher, Yu Huaiyuan.

Life back then was like a forced march on an oxygen-deprived plateau. The ten-minute breaks between classes were deathly silent in the classroom, with only fifty heads bowed over desks, greedily snatching sleep. Teacher Yu was the only "variable" in this stillness.

He wasn't tall, but was lean and agile, possessing a "worldly air" completely different from the traditional senior year homeroom teacher. In a system that prided itself on squeezing every minute, he stubbornly clung to a "near-luxurious" tradition—the monthly birthday party.

It was our monthly legal escape. Instruments played, otaku dances were performed. In those anxious days of recurring pandemics and uncertain return dates, the faint sweetness of cream became the only antidepressant. Teacher Yu would always buy a huge cake, have the birthday stars take photos and make wishes, and then, like a precise engineer, meticulously divide the sweetness among all fifty kids in the class. Even just one bite was enough for us to savor for a long time in the bitter sea of problems.

To help us survive this battle, Teacher Yu displayed remarkable "political wisdom."

At that time, the English scores in our class were the "biggest headache." Thus, English class entered an era ruled by a strict teacher we called the 'Empress.' This formidable teacher was swift and decisive, bombarding us with test papers, listening exercises, and newspapers in turn. Students from other classes even came to "borrow" our homework to get by. Faced with the "Wu Zetian's" dominance, Teacher Yu, a math teacher, chose "strategic deference." He tacitly allowed us to set aside math homework to focus entirely on English. This "Tian Ji's Horse Racing"-style survival philosophy actually worked, allowing our class's English scores to stage a comeback from rock bottom, carving out a bloody path.

However, life is like a book; turning the page is normal, but some pages you just can't turn past, and they become knots in your heart. On the eve of the college entrance exam, the pandemic led to a campus lockdown, and the dorms were operating at full capacity. The air was thick with the sweat and restlessness of youth. Teacher Yu became our patriarch. During nightly dorm checks, he saw the tacitly understood "contraband" phones in the boys' dorm but only gave a few perfunctory warnings. At that moment, we knew: Teacher Yu understood us. He was that kind of worldly, empathetic person.

But for me, memories of him are tinged more with a damp sense of guilt.

As a science student, while others were eloquently discussing conic sections and calculus, I was often tripped up and bruised by stumbling blocks like trigonometric functions. In chemistry and physics, I could confidently argue with teachers, even debating the correctness of a single step. But before math, I was too humble to lift my head. Every time I passed the math office, I would walk quickly with my head down, afraid of bumping into Teacher Yu.

My math scores perpetually struggled near the passing line; hitting triple digits was a divine miracle. That fear was concrete: I was afraid I wouldn't even get into an undergraduate program, afraid of becoming the school's first "disgrace" to attend a vocational college. I even wrote self-mocking doggerel about it. When handing out score slips, I would always cover the math column first, take a deep breath, brace myself mentally, and only then dare to glance at the abysmal score.

Finally, during a break in a late-night self-study session, the accumulated pressure burst its banks. I rushed into the office and cried like a lost child in front of Teacher Yu. "Teacher, I really can't learn it anymore. I'm hopeless." He didn't lecture or feed me platitudes. He just gathered us "math refugees" together and, on those nights of closed-off management, gave us extra tutoring.

In the empty classroom, the light was pure and warm. He stopped teaching those flashy, difficult problems. Instead, he picked up the chalk and started deriving from the most basic axioms. He devised the humblest yet most practical strategy for us: lose not a single point on low-difficulty problems, use basic methods to score on medium-level problems, and for those lofty, unreachable derivative and conic section problems, just write one formula and answer the first sub-question before decisively giving up, saving time to check the basics. "Don't be afraid. Hold onto the basic points, and you won't lose face."

During that time, chalk dust danced in the lamplight, like some kind of sacred dust. He wasn't teaching math; he was mending our tattered confidence.

The story ends, frozen at the farewell dinner. Faced with my dismal math score on the college entrance exam, I was filled with shame, feeling I had failed his late-night tutoring. I stood awkwardly before him, awaiting judgment.

But he didn't judge. He just reached out, patted my back heavily, and said a few meaningful words of blessing. The warmth of his palm penetrated my thin summer shirt. In that moment, all the shame and regret dissolved in this silent understanding.

After that parting, lost in the vast sea of humanity, we never met again. I only heard he's still on the podium, still carrying that same lean, worldly air.

Now, "Love Like Tide" in my headphones reaches its end. That unsung high note, that cake cut into fifty pieces, those basic formulas on the blackboard late at night, and that hand patting my back—they have all turned into the smoothest, warmest shells left on the beach after the tide recedes.

That was my seventeen. Though chaotic and filled with regrets, because of him, there was always light.

*Yu Huaiyuan is a pseudonym.
