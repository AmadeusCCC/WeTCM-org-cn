The lazy afternoon sunlight filtered through tattered curtains into the slumbering classroom.

In the classroom of the senior year science experimental class at Fuhai City's No. 17 Key High School, most heads were drooping, lulled into drowsiness by the math teacher's steady, hypnotic drone. On the blackboard, a complex spatial geometry problem sprawled like a monstrous creature, its chalk-drawn auxiliary lines resembling its dense, writhing tentacles.

<p className="sj2">
Gao Kun frowned deeply, his gaze fixed on the scratch paper, the pen in his hand almost snapping under his grip. His paper was covered with rigorous formulas and auxiliary lines, as if he were deciphering a code that held the fate of the universe.

Zhou Chengqi, sitting beside him, was also drawing with his head down, but the lines flowing from his pen tip had nothing to do with the monster on the blackboard.

In the center of his scratch paper, an intricate dome, spiraling staircases, layered archways—a meticulously detailed Baroque architectural maze was taking shape. And at the heart of this grand hall constructed from pencil graphite, a girl, sketched with just a few simple strokes, sprang to life. She was an anomaly within this extremely ornate style. No shadows, no depth, her divine yet youthful outline was rendered purely through minimalist lines. Her hair, left blank to suggest brightness, appeared strikingly radiant amidst the intricate classical shadows.

Zhou Chengqi had no interest in the problem on the blackboard that had the top students in the class scratching their heads. Not because it was difficult, but because it lacked beauty.

"Chengqi, stop drawing, the teacher's watching," Gao Kun nudged Zhou Chengqi with his elbow, his voice barely a whisper.

Without looking up, Zhou Chengqi's pen traced an elegant arc on the paper, as if adding a flying buttress to his own maze.

"For the third part of this problem, I tried a few shortcuts, none worked. Have to brute-force the calculation," he replied equally softly. "If I were designing it, just changing two data points would make it less... ugly."

Gao Kun pursed his lips and said nothing more, turning back to wrestle with his own scratch paper. He knew that discussing things this guy wasn't interested in was like talking about ice to a summer insect.

Zhou Chengqi's gaze fell upon his creation. The Baroque shadows rendered by his pencil strokes were heavy as blocks of historical lead, while the outline of the golden-haired girl was so light it seemed she might float off the page at any moment. This extreme dissonance gave him a sense of scrutiny, almost divine.

"Should have a name, I suppose," he muttered, his pen hovering over a blank space.

But every word that popped into his mind felt like an ill-fitting, cheap suit, appearing both pretentious and ridiculous when draped over this drawing. He suddenly felt that names were truly a lazy form of labeling. Just like himself—in the school roster, he was a number; at home, a title; in the eyes of passersby, a vague silhouette of a youth. Everyone was in a hurry to stick a label on him, as if writing "top student" or "otaku" meant they truly understood his mind.

"You wouldn't want to be called something random either, right?" He gently rubbed the hem of the drawn girl's skirt with his fingertip, picking up a smudge of graphite dust. "What you're called isn't important. What matters is... I drew you."

He finally put away his pen, leaving the title area in that comfortable state of blankness he preferred.

They are undefined, therefore they are free.

The shrill bell for the end of class finally rang. The classroom erupted in cheers.

"Vacation time!"

"Seven days for National Day, awesome!"

The math teacher frowned and rapped on the podium. "Quiet! All of you, quiet!"

The cheers subsided somewhat, but the excited atmosphere was palpable.

"Don't slack off during the break. The three sets of practice papers I assigned must be completed, checked on the first day back." The teacher paused. "Also, be safe, avoid crowded places. It's almost the season for frequent infectious diseases again. And... never mind, you won't listen anyway. Class dismissed."

As soon as the words were out, the classroom erupted in joy once more.

Gao Kun had just stood up when he was surrounded by several classmates.

"Bro Kun, any plans for the break? A few of us are going to the aquarium, wanna come?"

"Gao Kun, my dad got tickets to a music festival, interested?"

"Bro Kun, my cousin opened an escape room, insider price, let's go?"

Gao Kun smiled, responding to each one with ease. His natural social skills made him a born social butterfly. He patted one on the shoulder, promised another "we'll see," and with a few words, kept everyone happy.

Zhou Chengqi had already packed his things, slung his backpack over his shoulder, and walked out of the classroom.

Soon after, Gao Kun caught up, and the two walked along the corridor towards the school gate.

The sun had begun its descent, no longer as fierce as in summer but gentle, casting the unique golden-red glow of autumn, even with a hint of dense texture. The red brick walls of the teaching building were edged with this light, and even the usually cold-looking windows, adorned with red banners proclaiming "Final Battle: College Entrance Exams," now reflected warm, bright spots. The air no longer carried the usual mix of chalk dust and cafeteria food smells. Instead, it held a dry scent, a blend of the burnt fragrance of fallen leaves and faint dust—the smell of autumn.

"How's that novel of yours coming along?" Gao Kun asked. "Last time you said you were revising the structure of chapter three."

An autumn breeze blew past, swirling down a few palm-sized plane tree leaves. Zhou Chengqi watched a withered leaf get crushed under his foot before answering flatly, "Finished revising. You? Still planning to submit?"

"Of course." Gao Kun smiled, the golden-red glow of the sunset outlining him softly. "I've already submitted to three magazines. Radio silence so far, but gotta try. What if an editor has good taste?"

Zhou Chengqi curled his lip. "I can't stand others dictating changes to my work. What do those editors know? They only measure everything by market and sales, they don't care about the work's intrinsic meaning."

"But if you don't publish, how do you know if readers will like it?" Gao Kun shrugged. "And maybe earn some manuscript fees, treat you to a good meal."

"I don't need readers." A hint of stubbornness tinged Zhou Chengqi's tone. "I write for myself, not to please anyone. If forced to choose between 'being recognized' and 'staying true to myself,' I choose the latter."

Gao Kun glanced at him, smiling and shaking his head. "You, still so self-admiring. But that's fine, at least you know what you want."

They reached the school gate. A black business sedan was already parked by the curb. The driver got out and respectfully opened the door for Gao Kun.

"I'll head off then." Gao Kun patted Zhou Chengqi's shoulder. "Come hang out during the break if you have time. Don't just mope at home alone."

"Mm." Zhou Chengqi nodded.

Gao Kun got into the car. The window slowly rose, separating two worlds.

Zhou Chengqi stood there, watching the car merge into traffic and disappear around the corner.

He recalled how Gao Kun had been surrounded by classmates earlier, that effortless ease, that natural affinity.

Gao Kun was a good person. That was undeniable. He was cheerful, humorous, willing to help others, never putting on airs. He could remember every classmate's name and preferences, knew whose family had issues, who was feeling down lately. He was the kind of person who could make friends anywhere.

But Zhou Chengqi also knew he was different from him.

Gao Kun liked crowds, enjoyed feeling needed. He was a born leader, accustomed to being at the center, accustomed to making decisions for others. Whereas he preferred being alone, immersed in the world he built. There were no compromises, no accommodations, no need to say things he didn't mean just to fit in.

They were friends, but destined for different paths.

Zhou Chengqi turned and walked towards the bus stop.

Behind him was the noisy school and dispersing crowd; ahead, empty streets and the impending holiday. Alone, it was just right.

The No. 11 bus home was packed with adults getting off work.

"Recently, the third Rim of the Pacific joint military exercise commenced in our country's East China Sea waters. This joint exercise marks the first time Zhongxia and Merica have participated together since the World War, drawing significant international attention..." The news anchor's monotonous tone, combined with the bus's swaying, lulled the weary people, exhausted from a day's work, into the drowsiness of dusk.

The bus compartment was filled with a mix of sweat, perfume, and boxed meal smells. Sunlight, thick enough to almost drip, lazily melted into the bus windows before spilling onto every tired seat inside.

Solar radiation itself holds no emotion, but when it spends 8 minutes and 18 seconds crossing one astronomical unit, encounters water vapor and dust in the atmosphere, refracts into violet, indigo, blue, green, yellow, orange, and red, presenting the colors of all things—that too is a rather romantic affair. Zhou Chengqi leaned against the empty seat beside him, squinting slightly, drowsy.

Consciousness drifted between thoughts, initially feeling sluggish, but gradually he felt his soul sinking deeper... and deeper... away from the coarse, fragmented reality floating on the surface...

Simultaneously, he could hear a voice, speaking a language he had never heard yet could understand.

"Though the universe has a beginning, it has no end—infinite..."

"Though stars also have beginnings, they meet their end by their own power—finite..."

"Those who deem themselves wise often prove the most foolish; history is replete with such examples..."

The voice grew clearer, but his consciousness was swept along by a massive torrent, irredeemably plummeting towards an abyss... deeper and deeper, the scent of belonging and attachment growing stronger...

Looking up amidst the fall, he suddenly saw a brilliant river of stars, and his own voice echoed in his ears.

"Child of man, even as the end times approach, do not forget, I am with you..."

Suddenly, Zhou Chengqi jolted awake.

Opening his eyes, dusk was already outside the window. The setting sun was sinking below the horizon, setting the sky ablaze in a bloody red.

"...The Rim of the Pacific joint military exercise currently underway near Hawaii entered its free-play phase today. Our 'Fuhai' destroyer performed excellently in the drills, completing multiple joint damage control and technical maintenance exercises with the Merican amphibious assault ship 'Essex'... It is worth noting that the deeply collaborative bioscience project between the two nations has now been preliminarily applied to military purposes, reportedly for deterrence in the increasingly tense situation with the Mesopotamian states..."

The bus radio continued, but the voice had changed to a different anchor.

Zhou Chengqi rubbed his eyes and looked out the window.

Unfamiliar streets.

He froze for a moment, then sat up straight, turning to look at the buildings rapidly receding outside. The bus had long passed his familiar neighborhood and was now heading towards an unfamiliar suburban area.

"Shit," he cursed under his breath, hurriedly pressed the stop button, and jumped off at the next stop.

The dusk-lit bus stop was empty, only his solitary figure stretched long under the streetlight. He checked his phone map; he'd need to walk several kilometers to get back to familiar territory.

He looked up at the setting sun on the horizon, sighed, raised his phone, and pressed the shutter towards the burning clouds.

"Sigh... missed my stop."

Zhou Chengqi wasn't one to complain much about good or bad luck. Whatever happened, it shaped who he was now, so no need for regret, only acceptance and moving forward again.

He put away his phone, took out his old MP3 player, put on the headphones, separating himself from the thickening night behind him, and silently walked towards home.

Night deepened. On the deserted suburban path, the No. 11 bus, no longer needing to stop at every station, sped along freely.

In the driver's seat was Shi Jin, currently making a living as a bus driver. Although the last passenger, that sleepy, confused kid, had just gotten off, he still had to follow procedure and drive the bus to the remote, desolate terminal station.

"This exercise coincides closely with our National Day holiday and marks the first joint exercise between the two world powers in recent years, potentially signaling a further easing of relations between the two nations..."

Shi Jin snorted coldly, scoffing at the nonsense on the radio. As a former intelligence officer, well-versed in the uses of media, he was acutely aware: lies, in this era, both in scope and quantity, had become unprecedentedly powerful due to the development of social media.

His thoughts then shifted away from the news broadcast. His mind, like a precise scanner, began reviewing the day's work.

Hundreds of ordinary passenger faces were rapidly filtered out. Finally, the image settled on a blond, blue-eyed "tourist." In Shi Jin's mind, that person's image was magnified, analyzed.

The wear patterns on the backpack straps indicated carrying heavy loads over time, but the location of the wear didn't match the stress points of a conventional travel backpack. The calluses on the inner side of the second finger joints weren't from writing or labor, more like from long-term gripping of some instrument requiring precise control. And the overly relaxed, even slightly contemptuous posture upon seeing a street-corner patrol officer.

A faint, almost imperceptible, cold smile tugged at Shi Jin's lips. That was the sense of superiority leaking from a second-rate intelligence operative's disguise.

The third one this week. Fuhai City was getting livelier and livelier.

Thinking back now, while relations between Yan and Mi seemed to be thawing on the surface, cracks beneath the ice had begun spreading even during his active service. The current situation, he couldn't be sure.

"Next, the weather: As the National Day Golden Week approaches, a large amount of cumulonimbus clouds has suddenly and inexplicably gathered over our city. Experts advise friends with travel plans..."

Shi Jin turned off the radio, muttering to himself,

"Looks like the weather's about to change."

The bus, carrying the starlight on its back, its feeble headlights like two fireflies, drove fearlessly towards the terminal shrouded in gathering clouds.

By the time Zhou Chengqi got home, the moon was already hanging over the willow branches.

The house wasn't large but was exceptionally quiet. Most furniture was covered with dust sheets, and the air held the scent of long enclosure.

He deftly took a ready-made meal from the fridge, heated it in the microwave, then sat alone at one corner of the large dining table, mechanically shoveling food into his mouth.

Loneliness washed over him like a tide.

After eating, he took out his phone and dialed the familiar number.

The other end was filled with chaotic background noise, mixed with the roar of various machines.

"Hey, Mom, off work yet?... Tomorrow's National Day. School's giving us two and a half days off, evening self-study the day after tomorrow... Yeah, senior year, pressure's definitely there. I usually read to relax... Don't worry, hasn't affected studies. Mock exams have been pretty stable... How about Dad?... Still busy? So you guys aren't coming back this National Day either..."

He played the part of the sensible, worry-free son, just like every day for the past ten years. Such ordinary calls were his only usual connection with family. And once again, he hid his true feelings—recently, he'd been somewhat obsessed with drawing and writing, barely listening in class, though it was true his grades hadn't slipped yet. Family... saying he didn't miss them would be a lie. Even now, like a child, he looked forward to the family reunion during New Year. But separated by the ethereal phone line, the故作老成的少年 had learned to suppress his emotions with numbness. In his heart, he wasn't exactly against hanging up sooner to dive back into the创作 that had hit a bottleneck these past few days.

"Everything's fine, going smoothly..." His mother on the other end paused, her voice suddenly rising, filled with barely suppressed joy. "Chengqi, some good news! After finishing overtime tonight, we're taking a flight back tomorrow! We can spend the entire National Day holiday at home!"

Zhou Chengqi froze.

His fingers gripping the phone tightened instantly, knuckles turning white from the force.

All his calm and maturity crumbled in that moment.

His voice carried a tremor and delight he himself hadn't noticed. "...Really? Tomorrow? You... are you sure?"

After receiving confirmation, he leaned against the cold wall, let out a long sigh of relief, and a long-absent smile spread across his face, like a child finally getting the candy he'd longed for.

"Okay... then I'll wait for you to come back."

Hanging up, Zhou Chengqi looked at the empty house and, for the first time, felt it was no longer cold.

He began imagining the scene of meeting his parents tomorrow, even started planning where to go during the holiday.

On the eve of the end, his heart was filled with the warmest anticipation for the future.
