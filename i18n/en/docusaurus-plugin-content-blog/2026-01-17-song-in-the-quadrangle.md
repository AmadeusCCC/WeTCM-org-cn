## Song in the Quadrangle
<p className="sj2">
Author's Note: In January 2026, the written portion of the Jiangsu Provincial General High School Academic Proficiency Qualification Examination arrived as scheduled. The cold wind still blew outside the window—another year of the Academic Proficiency Test. Looking at the date on the calendar, memories deep within seemed struck by a stone, rippling slowly across the lake of time.
</p>

<p className="sj2">
The General High School Academic Proficiency Qualification Examination, commonly known as the "Academic Proficiency Test" or "Xue Ce," is a highly symbolic milestone in high school life. Unlike the fierce, single-file battle of the Gaokao, it resembles more a solemn "coming-of-age ceremony," marking the completion of studies in certain subjects. Passing this exam means we have met the "factory standards" of senior high school, obtaining the qualification certificate for graduation. After that, high school life shifts entirely toward the higher-intensity selective battle. Precisely because the Academic Proficiency Test has a "pass/fail" nature, the preparation period left an unusual memory within the tense high school years.

In ordinary high school days, the air was always thick with the smoke of competition. The higher-ranked students were more accustomed to silently calculating their positions, secretly competing with their set goals. However, the atmosphere during the Academic Proficiency Test period was quite subtle. Since subject selection was already complete, those unchosen subjects had often been set aside for a long time and now had to be picked up again; or one had to grit their teeth and tackle the hard nuts they had once avoided.

It's not hard to imagine that this was a disaster for some students with severe subject imbalances. In the science class I was in, there were several "pure science students" who were adept in the worlds of math and physics but seemed at a loss, even "unable to move an inch," when facing Politics and History. The confidence they had while freely exploring the ocean of science vanished, replaced by gloomy clouds when confronting liberal arts exam points—their expressions, truly like swallowing a bitter melon, resembled zombies in *Plants vs. Zombies* accidentally eating garlic, both pained and helpless, amusing to behold.

For a "pseudo-science student" like me, who wasn't weak in liberal arts but only average in science, this was instead a relatively comfortable time. During those days, the three main subjects (Chinese, Math, English) and the science teachers seemed to have reached a tacit understanding: homework loads plummeted, and they even tacitly allowed those "hopeless" in liberal arts to get extra tutoring in the office. Usually, when I asked those "high-and-mighty" science masters for help with math and physics problems, I couldn't avoid some humblebragging; but now the tables had turned, and they had to ask us for help with Politics and History knowledge points. While the masters were burning their brains in the liberal arts office, I had rare leisure, even managing to sneak in writing some mundane accounts during gaps in evening self-study.

Looking back now, it's precisely these seemingly unfocused, straightforward, even slightly trivial writings that became the most important signposts for me to trace back my high school life. Every time I flip through them, I'm grateful that I recorded those moments back then. Without these words, a piece of the most vivid puzzle of my high school memories would likely be missing.

Although the Academic Proficiency Test isn't difficult, as a national-level exam, its specifications and procedures are no different from the Gaokao. Classrooms must be cleared, and other grades leave for vacation. Whenever this happened, we would collectively move to the laboratory building, using it as a temporary review camp. As the exam approached, whether it was the confident "pseudo-science students" or the "pure science students" resolved to "face certain death," most had reviewed to the point of numbness.

Late at night during evening self-study, the vast campus was empty and quiet. Only the lights from the laboratory building pierced through the thick black curtain of the winter night, appearing exceptionally lonely and cold. The usually noisy and lively teaching building, now sealed off for the exam, had its doors and windows tightly shut, sinking into complete silence.

Perhaps to dispel this desolation, or perhaps trying to break through the numbing pressure brought by the Academic Proficiency Test, some student—no one knows who—started singing loudly in the corridor. On normal days, the grade-level department teachers would have surely heard it and sternly stopped this behavior that disrupted morale. But during those special days, the teachers tacitly allowed this audacity. Looking from afar through the glass windows, the door of the grade-level department's temporary office was slightly ajar, with teachers chatting and laughing inside, as if isolating the singing outside in another world.

With this silent "pardon," the students grew bolder. With exchanged glances, a tacit understanding formed, and singing during breaks became a regular activity. The laboratory building was a typical "回" (huí)-shaped structure, with two rows of four-story buildings connected by corridors, enclosing a central courtyard. This natural acoustic structure led students to spontaneously form a "mountain song antiphonal singing" setup—one side of the building would sing a line, the other side would respond, back and forth, with waves of song swirling, colliding, and drifting leisurely in the courtyard between the buildings.

I'm not usually good at singing, but immersed in this scene, the热血 (hot blood) within me couldn't help but boil. I pulled a few close friends, and during a break, we rushed to the corridor. Many songs sung back then have since faded with the wind, but one still roars in my mind to this day—it was Imagine Dragons' "Believer."

It was an explosive moment. My friends and I first leaned against the railing on our side, belting out the first part at the top of our lungs:

"Taking my message from the veins!

"Speaking my lesson from the brain!

"Seeing the beauty through the——"

Singing to this point, we默契 (tacitly) fell silent in an instant, holding our breath, waiting for the response from the opposite building—

"PAIN!"

A powerfully penetrating high note, like an arrow, sliced through the cold winter night air, shooting over accurately from the opposite building! At that moment, my friends and I jumped up excitedly on the spot, our scalps tingling. In that instant, I felt I understood the tremor Boya felt when he heard Zhong Ziqi exclaim, "How towering, like Mount Tai!" Where in the world can one find a soulmate? The soulmate was right across the quadrangle building!

That roar ignited the whole scene. The two laboratory buildings instantly became our stage, with interactive waves of sound rising and falling:

"You made me a, you made me a believer, believer——"

"PAIN!"

"You break me down and build me up, believer, believer——"

"PAIN!"

"Oh! let the bullets fly, oh let them rain……"

It was truly a grand and wild "concert"! To this day, I still don't know which classmates were on the opposite side, responding to us with all their might. But at that moment, I truly experienced the intoxication Confucius felt upon hearing the *Shao* music, where "for three months he did not notice the taste of meat"—he never imagined music could reach such a state! The singing echoed in the enclosed courtyard, refracted and amplified by the winter walls, carrying the特有的 (characteristic) youthful roar and unrestraint, possessing both a metallic texture and a fiery heat. It was no longer just a pop song; it was a release after suppression, a resonance of youth. The lingering sound circled the beams, striking directly at the heart, creating the most fervent reverberation in that cold winter night.

Today, "Believer" still sits in my playlist. Whenever that familiar melody comes on shuffle, I'm instantly transported back to the corridor that winter. I think I may never experience such畅快淋漓 (thoroughly enjoyable) and毫无顾忌 (uninhibited) antiphonal singing again. Thinking of this, a touch of sadness inevitably rises in my heart. But then again, it's precisely these shining fragments that, because of the songs between those buildings, gilded that somewhat bitter and monotonous high school period with a永不褪色的 (never-fading) golden edge.
