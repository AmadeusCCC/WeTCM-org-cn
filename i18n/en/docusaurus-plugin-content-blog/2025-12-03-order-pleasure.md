## The Joy of Order
<p className="sj2">
These past few days, I've been immersed in factory-building games, to the point of forgetting all else. Unlike entertainment filled with combat and competition, within the confines of this screen, I find a unique kind of tranquility and satisfaction. Watching raw materials flow along predetermined tracks, undergoing precise calculations and arrangements, and finally transforming into a dazzling array of finished products neatly stacked in warehouses, a sense of beauty in order arises—a feeling that everything can be measured and everything is under control. Pushing efficiency to its extreme is like dancing gracefully on the edge of a blade; is this not a feast of reason and logic?
</p>
<!-- truncate -->
<p className="sj2">
Take a game like *Cities: Skylines*, for example. Some are captivated by crafting magnificent skylines, while others are obsessed with amassing staggering wealth. What fascinates me most, however, is the seemingly troublesome and often overlooked aspect of traffic planning. How to lay roads, how to set up routes, where citizens will settle, where they will work and shop... all of this feels like diagnosing the circulatory system of a giant's body. When the intricate traffic network finally becomes smooth and unobstructed, the relief is akin to a doctor curing a chronic illness, clearing every clogged blood vessel.

This obsession with order and efficiency perhaps does not come from nowhere. It is like a deeply buried seed, quietly connected to my broader understanding.

Therefore, I have always held a cautious optimism towards industrialization and automation. I am well aware that its progress inevitably comes with growing pains and erodes certain inherent ways of life. Yet, looking at the long term, I still firmly believe that the scaled production that makes materials flow like a spring is an irreversible tide in the advancement of human civilization.

Tracing this belief back, one can always faintly glimpse a shadow of childhood.

Deep in my memory, by a country roadside, a relative ran a small workshop. It could hardly be called a factory—just a converted barn producing conical paper tubes for local textile mills. Including the owner couple, there were no more than ten people, but the time I spent there as a child sketched for me the earliest picture of production.

The raw material was bundles of kraft paper, occupying most of the workshop space. In one corner stood a mountain of paper, next to the paper-cutting machine. The hydraulic press drove the die down with a crisp, sharp friction sound, and the kraft paper snapped. Paper scraps were neatly pressed under the die; we children only dared to peek from afar. The first step was flattening the paper, making it pliable. Next came rolling and shaping. This was done in a small partition. A worker would attach the straight edge of a fan-shaped paper piece to a rotating, spire-like mold. The machine started, the mold spun rapidly, and the paper obediently wrapped around it. The mechanism below timely applied glue, and after a few more spins to press it tight, with a "thump," a formed paper cone was ejected. Accompanying this rhythm was the occasional "putt-putt" of the air compressor in the corner, which always gave me quite a fright back then.

However, what fascinated me most was the third step: "finishing." This was also the step we children were often called upon to help with. It was a machine with two chain conveyors. We placed the semi-finished paper cones onto the chains, and they began their marvelous journey. In a powerful industrial symphony, the cones passed in sequence: a rotating brush dressed them in an "outer coat," warm air dried them, a rotating grinding head polished their tips, and finally, the mechanism deftly flicked the finished product into a basket. All these actions were interlinked seamlessly, without a moment's hesitation or a single superfluous movement, presenting a beauty of fluid, uninterrupted execution.

The most wondrous part was that this entire complex machine was driven by a single motor, relying on the ingenious mechanical linkage of flywheels, gears, and cams ⚙️, not any smart chips. To speed up or slow down, one only needed to adjust the motor's speed. Every action was meticulously designed, meshing with the others. Even during drying, the mold holding the paper cone would rotate on its own to ensure uniformity. I often thought back then: if a giant conveyor belt could link all steps starting from the kraft paper, workers wouldn't have to haul forklifts around the workshop; if it could run completely without human intervention, how wonderful that would be.

From virtual games to dusty childhood memories, that thread of appreciation for efficiency and automation seems to have run subtly throughout.

My thoughts drift back to the present. It's quite cold tonight. When I went out for dinner, I suddenly craved the taste of the puffed rice cakes from my childhood. The loud "bang" that pierced the winter silence, along with the aroma wafting from the artisan's blackened rotating furnace, has been absent for a long time. Back then, whenever we heard this thunderous sound, family members would grab some rice and follow the noise to buy a furnace of fragrant, slightly charred rice cakes, which could last for days. Since the artisans were itinerant, bringing rice cakes when visiting relatives and friends added a unique flavor.

I suppose such craftsmanship is likely to be lost? Just as I was feeling melancholic about this possible disappearance, I inadvertently searched on an e-commerce platform—and found it has been industrially produced, and with impressive sales! This truly astonished me. So, what I thought was quietly fading away has, in fact, transformed and merged into this torrent of automation and industrialization.

Thus, the mechanical roar from the childhood workshop, the planning feast in the game, and the product link on the screen at this moment suddenly connected. I realized with a start that this simple joy in order, efficiency, and the beauty of automation has long been deeply rooted in my heart, subtly influencing me, culminating in tonight's reflections.
