## The Joy of Order
<p className="sj2">
These past few days, I've been immersed in factory🏭 building games, to the point of forgetting all else. Unlike entertainment filled with conflict and competition, within this small screen, I find a unique sense of tranquility and satisfaction. Watching raw materials flow along predetermined tracks, undergoing precise calculations and arrangements, and finally transforming into a dazzling array of finished products neatly stacked in warehouses, a sense of beauty in order arises—the feeling that everything can be measured and everything is under control. Pushing efficiency to its extreme is like dancing gracefully on the edge of a blade; is this not a feast of reason and logic?
</p>
<!-- truncate -->
<p className="sj2">
Take a game like *Cities: Skylines*, for example. Some are passionate about crafting magnificent skylines, while others are obsessed with amassing staggering wealth. What fascinates me most, however, is the seemingly troublesome and often overlooked aspect of traffic planning. How to lay roads, how to set up routes, where citizens will live, and where they will work and shop... All of this feels like diagnosing the blood vessels of a giant's body. When the intricate traffic network finally becomes smooth and unobstructed, the relief is akin to a doctor curing a chronic illness, clearing every clogged vessel.

This obsession with order and efficiency perhaps does not come from nowhere. It is like a deeply buried seed, quietly connected to my broader understanding.

Therefore, I have always held a cautious optimism towards industrialization and automation. I am well aware that its progress inevitably comes with growing pains and erodes certain inherent ways of life. Yet, looking at the long term, I still firmly believe that the scaled production that makes materials flow like a spring is an irreversible tide in the advancement of human civilization.

Tracing this belief back, one can always faintly glimpse a shadow of childhood.

Deep in my memory, by the roadside in the countryside, a relative ran a small workshop. It could hardly be called a factory—just a converted flat from an old livestock shed, producing conical paper tubes for local textile mills. Including the owner couple, there were no more than ten people in the workshop, but the time I spent there as a child sketched for me the earliest picture of production.

The raw material was bundles of kraft paper. Most of the workshop space was occupied by them. In one corner stood a mountain of paper, next to the paper-cutting machine. The hydraulic press drove the die cutter down with a crisp, clean friction sound, and the kraft paper snapped accordingly. The paper scraps were neatly pressed under the die cutter, and we children only dared to peek from afar. The first step of the process was flattening the paper, making it pliable. Next was rolling and shaping the paper. This was done in a small compartment. A worker would attach the straight edge of a fan-shaped paper piece to a rotating, spire-shaped mold. The machine started, the mold spun rapidly, and the paper obediently wrapped around it. The mechanism below timely applied glue, and after a few more spins to press it tight, with a "thump," a formed paper cone was ejected. Accompanying this rhythm was the occasional "putt-putt" sound from the air compressor in the corner, which always gave me quite a fright back then.

However, what fascinated me most was the third step: "finishing." This was also the step we children were often called upon to help with. It was a machine with two chain conveyors. We placed the semi-finished paper cones onto the chains, and they began their marvelous journey. In a powerful industrial symphony, the paper cones sequentially passed under a rotating brush to put on their "coat," warm air dried them, a rotating grinding head polished their tips, and finally, the mechanism cleverly flicked the finished product into a basket. All these actions were interlinked seamlessly, without a moment's hesitation or a single superfluous movement, presenting a beauty of fluidity accomplished in one go.

The most wondrous part was that this entire complex machine was driven by a single motor, relying on the ingenious mechanical linkage⚙️ between flywheels, gears, and cams, rather than any smart chips. To speed up or slow down, one only needed to adjust the motor's speed. Every action was meticulously designed, meshing with each other. Even during drying, the mold holding the paper cone would rotate on its own to ensure uniformity. I often thought back then: if only a giant conveyor belt could connect all steps starting from the kraft paper, workers wouldn't have to pull forklifts around the workshop; if only no human intervention were needed at all, how wonderful that would be.

From virtual games to dusty childhood memories, that thread of appreciation for efficiency and automation seems to have subtly run through.

My thoughts drift back to the present. Tonight is quite cold. When I went out for dinner, I suddenly craved the taste of the puffed rice from my childhood. The "bang" that pierced the winter silence, along with the aroma wafting from the artisan's blackened rotating furnace, has been long absent. Back then, whenever we heard this thunderous sound, family members would grab some rice and follow the noise to buy a furnace of fragrant, crispy rice cakes, which could last for days. Since the artisans were itinerant, bringing rice cakes when visiting relatives and friends added a unique flavor.

I suppose such craftsmanship is likely to be lost, isn't it? Just as I was feeling melancholic about this possible disappearance, I inadvertently searched on an e-commerce platform—and found it has been industrially produced, with impressive sales! This truly astonished me. It turns out that what I thought was quietly fading away has already transformed and merged into this torrent of automation and industrialization.

Thus, the mechanical roar from the childhood workshop, the planning feast in the game, and the product link on the screen at this moment suddenly connected. I suddenly realized that this simple joy in order, efficiency, and the beauty of automation has long been deeply rooted in my heart, subtly influencing me, until tonight when I felt compelled to express it.
