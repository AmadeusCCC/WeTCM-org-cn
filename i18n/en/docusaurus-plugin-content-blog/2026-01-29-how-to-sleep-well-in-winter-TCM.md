## 🌐 Learning from Traditional Chinese Medicine: How to Sleep Most Nourishingly in Winter
<p className="sj2">
This article is a repost.

Sleep is the primary way to eliminate physical fatigue. Properly regulating sleep is beneficial for nurturing both the body and the mind. Elderly individuals who enjoy health and longevity all have good and normal sleep. Winter sleep emphasizes going to bed early and waking up late. Sleep greatly impacts a person's health; poor rest can directly affect digestive function, the nervous system, physical recovery, and more.

### Optimal Sleep Time

    The optimal sleep time should be from Hai hour (9 PM to 11 PM) to the end of Yin hour (3 AM to 5 AM), meaning going to bed at 9 PM and waking up at 5 AM. During Hai hour, the San Jiao meridian is active, and the San Jiao connects all meridians. Entering a sleep state at this time allows all meridians to rest and recuperate, which can help a person avoid major illnesses throughout life. A common characteristic among centenarians is sleeping during Hai hour and waking during Yin hour. Traditional Chinese Medicine theory also states: "The gallbladder is the official of uprightness; the five zang and six fu organs depend on the gallbladder." The gallbladder is also Shaoyang, and "if Shaoyang does not rise, the world is not bright." If one does not go to sleep on time at night or has poor sleep quality, the Shaoyang qi will not rise the next day, making a person prone to drowsiness and lack of energy. In addition to ensuring good sleep at night, it is also advisable to arrange half an hour of sleep during Wu hour (11 AM to 1 PM) at noon.

### Staying Up Late at Night Easily Harms the Gallbladder and Can Lead to Depression

    Since human cells renew approximately every 100 days, ancient health practitioners said: "Sleep is the foremost method of health preservation; missing one night's sleep cannot be compensated for in a hundred days." Furthermore, frequently staying up late at night can harm gallbladder qi, and in severe cases, lead to depression. The *Yellow Emperor's Inner Canon* states: "Qi strengthens the gallbladder." "The eleven organs depend on the gallbladder." The qi of the five zang and six fu organs in the human body all depend on the gallbladder, relying on the generation and ascent of gallbladder qi. If gallbladder qi can be generated and ascend, the body will not be adversely affected. The period from 11 PM to 1 AM is Zi hour, when the gallbladder meridian is most active. The body accumulates and nourishes gallbladder qi during sleep; not sleeping consumes gallbladder qi, which in severe cases can lead to "depression" and a lack of courage in actions.

    Not sleeping during Zi hour, besides being unfavorable for bile metabolism, can also cause anemia and insufficient blood supply. Gallbladder deficiency leads to unclear vision above, and blood deficiency fails to nourish the sinews below, resulting in symptoms like eye fatigue, mental weariness, and soreness and weakness of the waist and knees. The liver and gallbladder correspond to the color green in the five elements. Not sleeping during Zi hour can easily cause a greenish complexion, and not sleeping during Chou hour (1 AM to 3 AM) can lead to an ashen complexion. The lungs correspond to white. Not sleeping until the end of Yin hour can easily cause a bluish-gray complexion.

### Waking Time Should Follow the Natural Rhythm

    3 AM is the "Beginning of Spring" of the day, and 6 AM is the "Spring Equinox" of the day. Heaven and earth awaken at 3 AM, and the body's cells also awaken under the influence of this spacetime. If one wakes between 3 AM and 5 AM daily, there is no need to sleep more; sometimes, sleeping further can make one more tired. If heaven is awake but a person does not get up, it is like a "tug-of-war," and humans cannot win against heaven and earth. If one has not gotten up by 5 AM, by 6 AM "Spring Equinox," the spirit cannot emerge, leading to insufficient vitality. If one goes to bed too late at night, it depletes yang qi; if one gets up too late in the morning, it suppresses yang qi. This is called "double depletion."

### The Sleeping Room Should Not Be Too Large

    The room for sleeping should not be too large, mainly to gather yang qi. Traditional health preservation emphasizes that doors and windows must be closed when sleeping. No matter how hot it is in summer, do not sleep with fans or air conditioning on. When a person falls asleep, a protective layer of yang qi forms on the body's surface. If wind disperses this layer of yang qi, the body will replenish it. Repeatedly draining yang qi in this cycle leads to feeling weak all over upon waking in the morning, a sallow complexion, and a head feeling wrapped in cloth. If it is too hot in summer, you can close the bedroom door, turn on the air conditioner to cool the room, take a quick shower, and then go to sleep promptly. The cool air can last for over an hour, by which time the person is already asleep. If one wakes up due to heat in the middle of the night, this method can be repeated.

### Dinner Before Bed Should Not Be Too Full

    If dinner is too full, it will inevitably increase the burden on the stomach and intestines. The information of their intense work is continuously transmitted to the brain, leading to insomnia and excessive dreaming. Over time, this can easily cause diseases like neurasthenia. If middle-aged and elderly people consistently overeat at dinner, repeatedly stimulating excessive insulin secretion, it often places a heavy burden on insulin B cells, leading to their exhaustion and inducing diabetes. At the same time, if dinner is too full, some proteins cannot be digested and absorbed. Under the action of intestinal bacteria, toxic substances are produced. Coupled with slowed intestinal peristalsis during sleep, which relatively prolongs the stay of these substances in the intestines, it may promote the occurrence of colorectal cancer. Therefore, dinner should not be too full, and overeating or binge drinking should be avoided, as this can easily induce acute pancreatitis, causing shock during sleep. If rescue is not timely, it can often be life-threatening.

    Additionally, eating too much for dinner consumes more qi. Consuming qi requires moving qi, which disturbs yang qi. Insufficient yang qi at night may lead to indigestion, food accumulation in the stomach, which over time transforms into heat, easily producing stomach heat. Excess yang leads to heat, resulting in poor sleep and affecting sleep quality. Therefore, not eating too much for dinner is also a good method for achieving the most nourishing sleep.
